# Musicianship Pedagogy Research
_Conducted 2026-05-22. Focus: how to track and incorporate musicianship into private piano lessons. Frameworks: Kodály, Gordon MLT, Feierabend Conversational Solfège, Dalcroze, Suzuki._

---

## Key Frameworks

### Kodály Method
- Voice is the primary instrument — singing should precede and accompany instrumental training
- Experience before notation ("prepare → present → practice" cycle)
- **Movable Do solfège** with Curwen hand signs for inner hearing
- Rhythm syllables: "ta" (quarter), "ti-ti" (paired eighths)
- Pentatonic before diatonic: Sol-Mi → add La → add Do → add Re → full pentatonic → then Fa and Ti
- Designed for classroom; adapted for private lessons with friction (piano methods don't align with Kodály sequencing)

### Gordon Music Learning Theory (MLT)
- Central concept: **Audiation** — hearing and comprehending music in the mind when not physically present
- Basic units are **tonal and rhythm patterns** (not individual notes) — like words vs. letters
- **Skill Learning Sequence (9 levels):** Aural/Oral → Verbal Association → Partial Synthesis → Symbolic Association → Composite Synthesis → Generalization → Creativity/Improvisation → Theoretical Understanding
- Uses **movable Do with La-based minor** (La = tonic of minor keys)
- **Music Moves for Piano** (Marilyn White Lowe) is the primary MLT-based piano curriculum
- Best suited for private lessons of any major framework

### Feierabend Conversational Solfège (CS)
- Bridge between Gordon (audiation) and Kodály (solfège)
- **12-stage sequence:** Readiness/Aural (1–5) → Reading (6–8) → Writing (9–12)
- Starts with Do-Re-Mi (unlike Kodály's Sol-Mi start)
- Well-suited to instrumental use; no hand signs required (uses "hand staff" instead)
- Each stage has four sub-phases: Rote → Decode Familiar → Decode Unfamiliar → Create

### Dalcroze Eurhythmics
- Rhythm is embodied, not abstract — body internalizes beat through movement, not counting
- Kinesthetic awareness (walking, swaying) improves rhythmic accuracy
- Clapping and tapping deliberately avoided — continuous movement preferred
- Contribution to private lessons: movement for macro/microbeat, arm gesture for phrase shape

### Suzuki
- Mother tongue principle: listen → play by ear → read notation
- Ear training is implicit (through repertoire exposure), not explicit (no systematic solfège)
- Gap: Suzuki students have excellent repertoire ears but weak sight-reading and functional ear training

## Solfège Systems

### Movable Do (recommended for functional ear training)
- Do = tonic of whatever key you're in
- The syllable encodes **function**, not absolute pitch
- Vowels encode tonal tendency: "oh" (Do, Sol) = stable; "ee" (Mi, Ti) = upward pull; "ah" (Fa, La) = downward resolution
- Used by Kodály, Gordon, Feierabend, and virtually all American functional ear training educators
- Consensus: significantly more useful than Fixed Do for developing ability to play by ear, sight-sing, and transpose

### Fixed Do
- Do = C always, regardless of key
- Encodes absolute pitch, not function
- Used in European conservatory tradition; useful for absolute pitch development before age 6
- Not recommended for functional ear training

### La-Based Minor (recommended)
- Natural minor scale: La Ti Do Re Mi Fa Sol La
- When moving from C major to A minor, Do stays on C; La = tonic of minor
- Preserves consistent half-step locations
- Used by Gordon (MLT) and Feierabend CS
- Alternative (Do-based minor) moves Do to the tonic of minor, using modified syllables (Me, Le, Te)

### Scale Degree Numbers
- Complementary to solfège, not competing
- Better for harmonic analysis (I chord, V chord) and jazz/pop contexts
- Less singable; no vowel-encoded tendency information

## Functional Ear Training: What It Is and Why It Matters

**Functional ear training:** Identifying how a note/pattern relates to the tonic of a key — what is its role?

**Interval ear training:** Identifying the distance between two notes in isolation (major third, perfect fifth, etc.)

**Why functional is more valuable:** Real music presents notes as events within a tonal field, not as isolated interval pairs. Identifying that a sound is "a perfect fifth" doesn't help you hear its gravitational pull in a key. Identifying that it's "Sol" immediately tells you it's stable, wants to resolve, sits above Do. Gordon's tonal pattern research supports this directly.

## Typical Progression: Beginner to Advanced

| Stage | Focus | Activities |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Pitch matching and echo | Pure imitation, no labels yet; develop in-tune singing and musical memory |
| 2 | Sol-Mi (Kodály) or Do-Re-Mi (CS/Gordon) introduction | Echo with named syllables; hand signs optional |
| 3 | Full pentatonic | Echo, improvise, identify Do-Re-Mi-Sol-La; Fa and Ti withheld |
| 4 | Fa and Ti; full diatonic scale | Full scale singing, diatonic echo, simple sight-singing |
| 5 | Harmonic context | Tonic vs. dominant feel; Do-Mi-Sol vs. Sol-Ti-Re patterns; phrase questions vs. answers |
| 6 | Minor and modal | La-based minor; major vs. minor discrimination; Dorian, Mixolydian for advanced |
| 7 | Advanced functional harmony | Scale degrees by quality; subdominant function; melodic dictation; sight-singing |

## Echo / Imitation Activities

The pedagogical foundation of every method. Structure:
1. Teacher sings a pattern (2–8 notes, solfège or neutral syllables)
2. Student echoes immediately
3. Repeat with variation
4. Role reversal (student creates, teacher echoes)

**Variations:**
- Echo with solfège labels (sing on "bah," then teacher names the solfège)
- Echo with Curwen hand signs
- Echo with notation (show where pattern sits on staff)
- "Finish my phrase" — teacher sings incomplete pattern, student completes
- Error detection — teacher sings with deliberate wrong note, student identifies
- Tonal vs. atonal contrast — student identifies which pattern "felt right"

**Pedagogical note:** Students need a large pattern vocabulary before notation is introduced. Extended echo phases are prerequisite, not time wasted.

## Practical Activities for Private Lessons

### Music Elements Awareness (without jargon)
- "What's the most important voice right now?" → melody/foreground
- "What's the lowest note doing — moving or staying still?" → bass awareness
- "How does the left hand feel — busy or calm?" → accompaniment texture
- "Does this feel settled or like it wants to go somewhere?" → harmonic tension
- "Can you hear when the music changes?" → form/phrase awareness
- Teacher plays melody, student plays bass simultaneously (then swap) — powerful for texture awareness
- "Listen while I play — don't look at the music — what do you notice?" → directed listening

### Music Horizon Broadening
- Play a 30-second clip related to what the student is working on (same composer, era, style)
- Ask a listening question before playing: "Is this heavy or light?"
- One or two sentences of biographical context — not a lecture
- Connect genres to student's existing interests
- Typically 1–3 minutes per lesson; habit, not unit

### Vocal Range by Age
| Age | Approximate Range |
|---|---|
| 5–6 | C4 to C5 (one octave) |
| 7–8 | A3 to G5 (~tenth) |
| 9–10 | A3 to D5–G5 |
| 11–12 | A3 to C6 |

**Practical implications:**
- Start solfège patterns in the middle of range, not the bottom (Sol-Mi-La, not ascending from Do)
- Cultivate head voice; chest-dominant singing limits range
- Don't push range expansion before puberty
- If student can't match pitch: match their pitch first, then move incrementally upward

### Time Allocation
- **Minimum viable:** 3–5 min per lesson (the "3-minute ear training" model from Colourful Keys)
- **Recommended for real progress:** 8–10 min per lesson
- **Best approach:** Integrated, not blocked — open with 2–3 min of echo/solfège, connect it to repertoire, close with 1–2 min directed listening

## Tracking Vocabulary for a Lesson System

### Solfège Fluency
- System used: Movable Do (La-based minor)
- Syllables introduced: Do Re Mi Fa Sol La Ti (track each)
- Pentatonic complete: yes/no
- Diatonic complete: yes/no
- Minor introduced: yes/no

### Tonal Skill Level (per Gordon MLT)
For each tonal concept: **None → Aural/Oral → Verbal Association → Symbolic Association**

### Rhythm Skill Level (per Kodály/MLT)
For each rhythm concept: **None → Aural/Oral → Verbal Association → Symbolic Association**

### Echo / Imitation
- Pattern length handled: 2-note / 4-note / 8-note
- Echo accuracy: unreliable / emerging / reliable
- Student can generate patterns: yes/no

### Singing in Lesson
- Pitch matching reliable: yes/no
- Sings repertoire on solfège: yes/no
- Sight-singing readiness: not yet / emerging / yes

### Functional Listening
- Identifies melody vs. bass: yes/no
- Hears harmonic change: yes/no
- Major/minor discrimination: yes/no
- Tonal center identification (can find Do): yes/no

### Music Horizon
- Directed listening done this lesson: yes/no
- Genres exposed: list
- Composers mentioned: list
- Student-initiated curiosity: free text

## ⏸ Deferred Musicianship Items (Out of Scope Now)

- Melodic dictation (hearing and notating a melody) — requires sight-singing readiness first
- Formal RCM-style ear training benchmarks
- Sight-singing as a standalone activity (emerges from solfège progression)
- Rhythm dictation
- Harmonic dictation
- Modal ear training (Dorian, Mixolydian) — for advanced students only
- Student-facing practice apps for echo/solfège drill (EarMaster, ToneDear, etc.) — Layer 2
